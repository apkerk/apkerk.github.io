Research Overview

I am a PhD candidate in Organizational Behavior at Cornell University. My research examines the social dynamics of entrepreneurship, work, and gender inequality, with a focus on how digital platforms and emerging technologies reshape opportunities and outcomes.

My primary research stream focuses on new forms of digital entrepreneurship such as content creation and social media influencing. Using organizational theory and sociological perspectives, I explore how digital platforms, algorithms, and social media create new opportunities and constraints for founders, and the implications for inequality, innovation, and the future of work.

Specifically, my research investigates:

  • New forms of work enabled by digital technology
  • The origins and consequences of gendered patterns in digital entrepreneurship - including the types of ventures founders pursue, their founding identities, the strategic behaviors they adopt, and the outcomes they achieve
  • The social dynamics driving these patterns

My work is relevant to scholars interested in the social dimensions of entrepreneurship, technology, innovation, labor markets, and organizational theory. I employ econometric methods, including difference-in-differences designs, alongside qualitative research methods.

Prior to academia, I founded and exited a digital marketing asset company and earned an MBA from Brigham Young University. I have taught courses on the Sociology and Psychology of Work at Cornell and MBA business courses at BYU.

Job Market Paper

Approval Without Amplification

Women get approval, men get amplification.

My job market paper asks how gender inequality operates in platform entrepreneurship when audience reception and visibility are linked through recommendation systems. I analyze a random sample of 9,232 U.S. YouTube creators and more than 6 million comments. Women's channels receive about 33% fewer views in the raw data. About a third of that gap reflects production differences, including younger channels and fewer videos. Holding constant channel age, video count, HD status, channel-trailer use, and comment volume, the remaining gap is about 22%.

That remaining gap is not explained by topics, titles, transcripts, or thumbnails. Instead, the pattern points to how audiences engage with the content. Women's channels receive more positive and less profane comments, higher engagement, and no comparable controlled subscriber gap. Positive comment tone is associated with fewer views and profanity with more views: comment tone and profanity account for most of the controlled view gap, suggesting those softer audience signals are less rewarded by human resharing and an inferred engagement-optimizing recommender system.

Background

I am a PhD candidate in Organizational Behavior at Cornell University's ILR School, where my dissertation is chaired by Pamela Tolbert, with Ben Rissing and Wesley Sine on the committee. I study digital entrepreneurship, creators, and platform-based work, with gender as the empirical lens.

The dissertation runs across three chapters. The first, my job market paper, shows how platform algorithms shape gendered evaluation and visibility, so that women's channels can draw warmer reception yet less of the amplification that turns into views. The second is an interview study of digital-era founders, built from 61 qualitative interviews, that identifies an Individualist founder type who organizes the venture around an idealized self rather than an external market opportunity. The third is a conceptual chapter that frames content creation in the creator economy as a distinct new form of digital venture creation.

I combine econometric methods, including difference-in-differences designs, mediation, and sensitivity analysis on large original datasets, with qualitative interviewing. Before academia I founded, operated, and sold a digital marketing asset company, and I earned an MBA from Brigham Young University. At Cornell's ILR School I designed and taught my own weekly discussion sections for "Psychology of Work" and "Sociology of Work," and I have assisted with MBA and Executive MBA courses at Cornell's Johnson School of Business and BYU's Marriott School of Business.
